Negotiation & Historical Context

The Gathering at Lower Fort Garry

In August 1871, over 1,000 Anishinaabe and Muskego-Cree people gathered at Lower Fort Garry to negotiate with the Crown . The atmosphere was tense; the First Nations leaders, including Chief Mis-koo-kenew (Red Eagle), were not merely "surrendering" land but seeking a framework for coexistence and protection against the rapidly expanding waves of settlement.

The "Outside Promises" Controversy

A defining feature of Treaty 1 is the discrepancy between verbal and written terms. While the written document focused on land cessation, the First Nations only signed after receiving verbal "Outside Promises" for:

  • Agricultural supplies (plows, harrows, and livestock) to transition their economies.
  • Healthcare provisions and educational support for future generations.
  • A census to ensure accurate annuity payments.

These promises were not formally added to the written treaty until 1875.

Geographic Boundaries

Treaty 1 Territory

The territory covers approximately 16,700 square miles. Its borders extend from the US-Canada border north to the shores of Lake Winnipeg, including the fertile Red River Valley.

Treaty 1 in the 21st Century

Naawi-Oodena Urban Reserve

The Kapyong Barracks redevelopment, known as Naawi-Oodena, is a landmark example of Treaty implementation. It transforms former federal land into a massive multi-use urban reserve, providing a direct economic engine for all seven Treaty 1 nations within the city of Winnipeg.

Economic Sovereignty

Today, implementation focuses on Economic Development Zones. Examples include the Long Plain First Nation urban reserves, which host commercial hubs (like gas stations and office spaces), allowing Nations to exercise their Treaty rights to trade and land use in a modern economy.
